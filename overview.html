Understanding speech feels effortless. We process it in a fraction of a second, often without even realizing it. But in everyday life, communication sometimes breaks down. Imagine you are talking with a new friend at a noisy restaurant. If they have a foreign accent you’ve never heard before, you might struggle to understand them at first. However, over time, their speech often becomes easier to follow. It’s not necessarily that they start speaking more clearly. Instead, your ears learn to adjust to the way they speak.

My research explores how we can help people train their ears to understand speech more easily in difficult situations. For example, if you have tried to learn a new language, you might have come across sounds that seem completely the same. Imagine learning English and hearing “red” and “led” or “road” as “load” as identical. How difficult would it be for you to understand the meaning? But just like with a foreign accent, your ears can be trained to better hear such differences.

It appears that there are a set of principles we can follow to design training programs that use the least time and effort to improve your listening skills as you need. For example, ear training at night seems to be more effective than during the day, since you remember things better with sleep when no other sounds disturb you. Hearing five speakers with a foreign accent helps you understand a new speaker with the same accent better than hearing just one speaker. Principles like these guide us in designing optimal training programs that can be used for cross-cultural communication, second language learning, and even for supporting people with communication disorders.
